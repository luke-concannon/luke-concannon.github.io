A Beginners Guide to Solving Problems

Solving Problems, Since 1986


Now, I've been solving problems since day dot. Life is full of them. One after the other, they just keep coming at me. So I like to think I'm well practiced in solving problems. But in the quest to become a more proficient finder of solutions, here are some reflections on smashing through some problems in my first few weeks of coding....


Look here Object, give me that damn nested value!


This week I came across a steadfast roadblock in my javascripting. I was trying to dynamically insert into an Object some properties from two other arrays. The complexity came in the fact that the values from one array were to be nested in the object as keys, with the values of those key being nested objects (which were to include the corresponding values from the second array). I managed to loop through the arrays and successfully insert the first arrays values as keys in my object, but when trying to insert the second array's values as a nested object representing the values of the target Objects keys, I came completely unstuck!


TEST - FAIL - TEST - FAIL


I find this to be my default problem solving technique. With console.log() as my trusty sidekick, I can test and fail till the cows come home. They say that failing or making mistakes is one step closer to success. Yeh right, until it isn't! Testing things and failing is the nature of programming, and an important problem solving technique, but unless supported by other approaches, you will eventually meet problems that totally stump you!


GOBBLE UP THAT ONLINE CONTENT!


Having drained the well of my knowledge I then bash my problem into Google, open up a bunch of tabs and gobble up as much learning as I can in as short a time as possible. A tried and true approach to solving problems, and one that we all rely on daily. In this instance, no joy - I must have read 20 different articles on all the which ways I can loop through objects. Then I decided the loop wasnt actually the issue and that instead I'm just not using the correct notation to access the values I need. And herein lise an issue with relying on referencing others' content in trying to solve your problem, you are relying on 2 things - the fact that you know exactly what your problem is; and the fact that someone else has experienced this problem. I half knew what my problem was, and so couldn't really find resolution from others' experiences.


SEEKING HELP FROM A REAL PERSON


The single most effective way to tackle problems is by collaborating with other human beings. It is also sometimes the hardest thing to do. Maybe because we it feels like we're admitting failure in asking for the answer, or maybe it's just because we are ego driven creatures that find it difficult to admit to ourselves we are stuck. Either way, doesn't change the fact that asking a real human person will bring all the joy of any other problem solving technique 10 times over (caveat: most of the time). So this is what I did. I reached out to someone on the slack channel, they hit me up with some code and I laughed in the face of its elegance and simplicity. Truly, I had overcomplicated my problem to a comical degree.


The Scouts Guide to Problem Solving


PSEUDOCODE


Writing pseudocode is a great way to plan your approach. But there is pseduocode and there is pseduocode. Currently the pseduocode I write feels more like a description of what I think I should do rather than a specific list of code I will write - mainly because I don't have enough tools in my JS toolbox to draw on when planning my aproach. This will change with practice and up-skilling.


TRYING SOMETHING


This I am well versed in. It's my default problem solving approach. Have an idea, try it, fail, finesse the code, try it, fail...


RUBBER DUCKY METHOD


Explaining to your rubber ducky the problem, and potential solutions. I get it but am yet to try it. Sometimes I begin to explain puzzles to my girlfriend and I see the value - the rubber ducky (or love partner) has no sense of what you are talking about, so you have to break it down in novel ways to try to get them to understand. In this lies the value.


READING ERROR MESSAGES


Sometimes the error messages are hugely helpful e.g. when you are missing a curly bracket or calling on an undefined variable. Other times they are utterly not helpful, and can be downright abusive, with their red angry color and monospace font. grrrr.


CONSOLE.LOGGING


My friend, the console.log - A true companion in finding out what the hell your code is actually asking the computer to do! The console.log is like asking the computer for a little clue to the problem. It is a great way to debug and identify specific pieces of code and their affects.


GOOGLING


The world is a different place because of the internet, and search engines are an essential tool in accessing the information we need. A great learning tool in any industry, but a vital one for programmers - like the bible for Christians, search engines give us access the knowledge that will guide us to heaven!


ASKING YOUR PEERS FOR HELP


Hard to do, but hugely effective. This approach is used after all other approaches have failed. I think I could use it sooner and more regularly, but I also think that a little bit of struggle before doing this is important.


ASKING COACHES FOR HELP

IMPROVING YOUR PROCESS WITH REFLECTION


Reflection is hugely effective and can be immensely enjoyable. I feel well practiced in building a reflexive practice in my working life - in a previous incarnation as a teacher, reflection lay at the centre of our professional development and I think it should do for all people in all situations. I work with many people in factory settings that are not exposed to reflection, and when they get to experience it they feel stretched and challenged to consider their journey from the perspective opf the 'observer'.
